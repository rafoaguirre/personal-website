There's a version of technical writing that's essentially a tutorial with screenshots. That's useful, but it's not what I'm interested in doing here.

What I want to write about is *judgment* — the reasoning behind decisions, the trade-offs that don't show up in the final diff, and the things I got wrong before I got them right.

## Writing as a compression function

When I work through a problem in code, I end up with a solution that encodes a lot of implicit knowledge: why I chose this abstraction over that one, what alternatives I considered, what constraints I was working within. Most of that context disappears the moment I merge the pull request.

Writing forces me to surface that context and make it explicit. The act of explaining something — even to an imagined reader — reveals the gaps in my understanding faster than re-reading the code does.

## What I plan to write about

A few areas I keep coming back to:

- **Architecture decisions**: small decisions that have outsized downstream effects
- **TypeScript patterns**: things that work at scale vs. things that feel good in a tutorial
- **Cloudflare Workers and edge computing**: building for a distributed, stateless runtime
- **Engineering trade-offs**: when the "correct" answer depends on context that textbooks don't cover

## The compounding return

One more practical reason: writing compounds in a way that most other output doesn't. A good article can answer a question for someone two years from now. A well-explained decision can save a future teammate an hour of archaeology in the git history.

I don't know exactly what this will turn into. But I'm treating it as a long game, and I'm starting here.
